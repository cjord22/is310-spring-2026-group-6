# Instructor Feedback on Group & Individual Topic Selection

## Group Feedback

I appreciate your group's interest in US history and politics, and think it has the potential to be a rich focus for the course. Your group has chosen topics with genuine significance (immigration, women's suffrage, political influence, stock market dynamics, art history, and race relations), and each of these is a fascinating entry point for examining how culture, politics, and historical narratives are constructed and represented through data. However, your planning document needs significant development in a few key areas before you can move forward with confidence.

Here are my main observations:

1. **Connecting History to Cultural and Data Analysis**

You've selected topics that matter culturally and historically, but your current framing tends to emphasize the *historical events* ("what happened when") rather than the *cultural phenomena* they represent ("how meaning is constructed"). For this course, you need to explicitly articulate the **cultural questions** your data can help you answer.

Some examples of this reframing:

- Immigration isn't just a historical fact; it's about how societies construct narratives about belonging, identity, and economic value. What cultural attitudes toward immigrants are embedded in policy data, legislative language, or media coverage?
- Stock market crashes aren't just economic events; they reflect (and shape) cultural values around risk, greed, panic, and economic trust. What does the language surrounding crashes reveal about cultural anxieties of different eras?
- Women's suffrage involved competing narratives about women's roles, citizenship, and public participation. What cultural narratives are visible in how this movement was debated and represented?

**The key move:** Shift from "what happened" to "what does this tell us about cultural meanings, values, power, and representation?" Your data should illuminate cultural questions, not just historical timelines.

2. **Developing a More Coherent Group Theme**

Right now, your individual projects feel somewhat independent: immigration law changes, women's suffrage, stock market crashes, American art history, race relations, and women's political influence. You need to develop a stronger **conceptual thread** that ties them together and creates a shared analytical lens for your group.

It could be that you focus temporally more narrowly, or it might be methodologically (e.g., how different types of data reveal cultural narratives), or thematically (e.g., how power and representation are constructed in different domains). But you need to articulate this shared theme clearly. You could also think about a unifying question that all your projects address in different ways. For instance, are you interested in binaries in American culture (insider/outsider, citizen/non-citizen, valued/devalued)? Are you interested in how certain groups/events become visible/invisible in historical discourse? Are you interested in how cultural values are reflected in political and economic systems? Are you interested in the visual culture of these events? All of these could be interesting angles that would give your projects a shared focus and help you generate more coherent insights as a group.

1. **Clarifying Data Workflows**

Currently, it is difficult to assess your projects because the individual themes are very broad with limited datasets. I would highly encourage everyone to spend some time thinking about whether for this stage they plan to create data from scratch (e.g., by digitizing archival materials, transcribing texts, collecting from digital sources) or whether they will work with existing datasets (e.g., published archival datasets, digital humanities collections, existing research datasets) that need to be verified and augmented. Similarly, you all need to think a bit more about how computation and scale will assist you in this work. I am providing some thoughts and suggestions below for each student, but I would highly recommend spending some time to consider the assignment requirements and how they apply to your projects. 

1. **Engage with Published Scholarship**

Your topics sit at the intersection of history, political studies, public history, digital history, and information science. There is substantial scholarship examining how historical narratives are constructed, represented, and contested through data and discourse. I would encourage you to start engaging with key works in these fields early on, particularly those that critically examine historical representation, archival gaps, and how power shapes what gets documented.

Some starting works and projects to consider:

- Nelson, Laura K. “The Inequality of Intersectionalities in Chicago’s First-Wave Women’s Movement.” *Signs: Journal of Women in Culture and Society* 47, no. 4 (2022): 905–30. [https://doi.org/10.1086/718866](https://doi.org/10.1086/718866).
- *Mapping Color in History* [https://mappingcolor.fas.harvard.edu/](https://mappingcolor.fas.harvard.edu/). This project provides a searchable database of pigment analysis in Asian paintings, but also shows how scholars work with Museum materials and image analysis.
- *Mapping Inequality* [https://dsl.richmond.edu/panorama/redlining/](https://dsl.richmond.edu/panorama/redlining/). This project maps redlining in US cities and reveals how racialized policies shaped urban development and inequality. It also critically examines the archival sources used to create the dataset and the narratives they produce.
- *Mapping the Gay Guides*, Amanda Regan and Eric Gonzaba, (2019-): [http://www.mappingthegayguides.org](http://www.mappingthegayguides.org). This project maps the history of gay guides in the US and explores how queer spaces were represented and contested in print media.
- *On the Books: Jim Crow and Algorithms of Resistance* [https://onthebooks.lib.unc.edu/](https://onthebooks.lib.unc.edu/). This project uses computational methods to analyze the language of Jim Crow laws and how they were resisted.
- You might take a look at Stanford University Press' Digital Projects [https://www.sup.org/digital](https://www.sup.org/digital), which includes projects like Tilton, Lauren, Taylor Arnold, and Courtney Rivard. *Layered Lives: Rhetoric and Representation in the Southern Life History Project*. Stanford University Press, 2022. [https://doi.org/10.21627/2022ll](https://doi.org/10.21627/2022ll).

You should also look at journals like *American Historical Review*, *Journal of Digital History*, *New Media & Society*, and *Digital Humanities Quarterly* for work on historical narrative, public history, archival representation, and digital approaches to historical questions.

Since I am a digital historian, I would highly recommend reaching out to schedule a time to chat with me. I would also recommend potentially reaching out to Prof. Kalani Craig, in the History Department, who also works on these topics.

### Daria

You've identified a genuinely important topic—–immigration policy is a site where cultural values, political power, and historical change intersect in fascinating ways. The Immigration Yearbook 2019 is a strong starting point as a data source, and you clearly recognize that immigration law isn't just about logistics; it's about how nations define belonging and citizenship. However, you need to clarify several things to make this project feasible and analytically compelling.

First, how do you envision using the Immigration Yearbook data? Is it a source you can actually verify its data and methodology? When I downloaded some of the data, it seemed to be primarily aggregate counts, which makes sense since it is from the Department of Homeland Security. But that raises serious concerns for how will you verify this data, and assess what gets counted and what gets excluded. One angle might be something similar to this *Pudding* article on Census history ([https://pudding.cool/2020/03/census-history/](https://pudding.cool/2020/03/census-history/)), which is a great example of how to critically analyze the data collection process itself, and how categories and methods change over time. You should consider doing something similar with the Yearbook data: How was it collected? What categories are used? How have these changed over time? What does this reveal about what was valued or feared in different eras? It looks like the Yearbook data goes back to 1996, so you could try to analyze how the categories and data collection methods have changed over the past few decades, and what this reveals about shifting cultural attitudes toward immigration.

Such an approach might help you concretize your question of how have immigration laws changed, by showing how the data itself reflects changing cultural and political attitudes. To be clear though, immigration policy in the United States is very complex and historically contingent, so you would also want to be thoughtful about what drives these changes (e.g., economic conditions, political movements, international events) and how they intersect with cultural narratives about immigration. 

You also need to consider how you might augment this data. Some possibilities include augmenting the quantitative data with more qualitative textual sources like policy documents and legislative language to analyze *how* immigrants are described and categorized. There is a great digital history project looking at shifts in 19th-century legal language that might provide some inspiration: Funk, Kellen, and Lincoln A. Mullen. The Spine of American Law: Digital Text Analysis and U.S. Legal Practice (Annotated Version). 2021. [https://doi.org/10.31835/ma.2021.07](https://doi.org/10.31835/ma.2021.07).

You could also look at media coverage of immigration law, though that is a massive topic, so you might want to think carefully about how to narrow it (e.g., specific time periods, specific types of media, specific events). You could use news archives like Chronicling America [https://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov/](https://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov/) to try and see how certain laws get covered and what narratives are constructed around them. 

Or you might be very interested in how the government provides these resources and do more of a history of this Yearbook website and data collection process itself, which would be a fascinating project in its own right. You could look at how the Yearbook data was collected, how it has changed over time, and what this reveals about shifting cultural attitudes and also government practices around data transparency. 

Generally, though I think you need to really think carefully about what specific question you want to answer with this data, and how you will verify and augment it, as well as what is feasible within the scope of this course. Happy to discuss further if you want to chat about this!
