## Introduction

In the final project for Culture as Data, I studied immigration laws and the sentiments of their text spanning three states over time. Immigration laws qualify as cultural data because they preserve some of the historical moments that were specifically in this era. They also embed and intertwine with a specific culture of the population, and also with how other cultures mix together, since we are talking about immigration. Overall, history and culture always come together, even though culture is more something established over time and does not change rapidly. Still, history is one of the variables that shapes culture, and immigration law is exactly where that happens.

My central research question is which state — Illinois, California, or Arizona — is the most pro-immigration, based on the laws that were proposed in each of the states and how each state's stance shifted over time. My reasoning for choosing these three specific states was based on the controversy and contrast that each of them had. Also, one of my curiosities was exactly when Arizona became more of an anti-immigration state and what legislation marked that shift.

To answer this question, I built a dataset of 589 immigration laws across the three states spanning 2009 to 2026, sourced from four different databases: the National Conference of State Legislatures Immigration Legislation Archived Database, the LegiScan API, the California Legislative Information portal, and the Arizona Legislature public API. Second, I trained a logistic regression model on those features using my labels, and the model then assigned a probability score to each law, where a score closer to 1 means more pro-immigration and closer to 0 means more anti-immigration. What interested me most was not just finding which state scored highest, but seeing whether the words the model weighted most heavily matched the patterns I noticed when I was reading the laws myself.

## Section 2: How You Made It — The Manual Labeling Process

The labeling process was the part of this project that required the most judgment, and I want to be honest about exactly how it worked. I did not read the full text of every law from scratch. The NCSL database provides a short description of each law — usually one or two sentences explaining what the bill does — and that description is what I read for each of the 67 laws in the dataset. For a small number of laws, I had direct links to the Statenet full-text portal and was able to go deeper into the actual bill language, but for most of them the NCSL description was my primary source.

On top of that, I ran each law through both Gemini and Claude Browser using the same prompt, asking each model to summarize in one sentence what the law was doing for or against immigrants. I want to be clear about what role those AI summaries played. They were a reference tool that helped me understand unfamiliar legislative language more quickly — not the source of my labels. I read the AI output, compared it to the NCSL description, made my own decision, and assigned the Pro or Anti label myself. If the model framed something differently than I read it, I went with my own interpretation. For 21 of the laws I read the full bill text and wrote a reasoning note explaining my decision for each one — not just the borderline cases, but every law in that set. For the remaining laws I worked from the NCSL description and did not record written reasoning for every entry.

My rubric for Pro vs. Anti was based on the intent and effect of the law, not just its surface language. I defined a Pro-Immigration law as one that expands rights, protections, or access for immigrants — for example, laws that limit cooperation between state law enforcement and ICE, laws that protect immigrants in sensitive locations like schools and hospitals, or laws that extend state benefits or due process rights to undocumented residents. I defined an Anti-Immigration law as one that expands enforcement against immigrants, restricts their eligibility for something, increases penalties tied to immigration status, or deepens the state's cooperation with federal enforcement mechanisms. The reason I focused on intent and effect is that legislative language can be deliberately vague. A law that says "clarifies reasonable suspicion requirements for immigration checks" sounds neutral until you realize it was written to protect Arizona's SB 1070 from legal challenge — which makes it very clearly Anti-Immigration once you understand the context.

There were several borderline cases that I had to sit with before I could label. One of them was California's CA S 836, an evidence law from 2022 that dealt with immigration status in court proceedings. The description I wrote was that immigrants should be able to participate in open court unless the judge requires otherwise, and I labeled it Anti-Immigration because the "unless the judge requires" clause felt like a restriction — it created a carve-out where a judge could remove an immigrant from their own court proceedings based on status. Another borderline case was Arizona's AZ S 1376 from 2023, which appropriated money to the Department of Emergency and Military Affairs and specifically designated funds for an Anti-Human Trafficking Grant Fund. On the surface, human trafficking protection sounds like it is helping immigrants. But when I read it more carefully, the enforcement framing — funding military and emergency affairs specifically to intercept undocumented crossings — made it fall on the Anti side. The funding was about border enforcement, not immigrant protection. That is the kind of distinction that I could only make by actually reading the context, not just the title. A third borderline case was Arizona's AZ S 1350 from 2017, which expanded Arizona's terrorism definition to include actions connected to immigration enforcement. The word "terrorism" in relation to immigration could be read as targeting human traffickers, which sounds like it protects immigrants. But the expansion of what the state classifies as terrorism in an immigration context — extending the reach of the enforcement apparatus — made it fall on the Anti side once I read past the title.

The hardest single law to label was probably AZ H 2162 from 2010, which was an amendment to SB 1070 — the most famous and most controversial immigration law Arizona ever passed. The amendment's stated purpose was to clarify that law enforcement could not consider race, color, or national origin when making immigration stops. I went back and forth on this one more than any other law in the dataset. On one reading, the amendment was written specifically to shield SB 1070 from racial profiling lawsuits, which makes it feel like a legal defense of enforcement rather than a genuine protection. On another reading, the explicit prohibition on using race or national origin during a stop creates a real legal constraint on how officers can conduct immigration checks, and that constraint does protect immigrants from one specific type of discriminatory practice. What finally pushed me toward Pro-Immigration was that the prohibition itself, if applied, limits what officers can do during a stop — and limiting enforcement is the direction my rubric pointed to, regardless of what motivated the legislature to write it. That decision still feels uncertain to me, and it shows up as one of the six laws the model got wrong in LOO validation, where it predicted Anti-Immigration.

Overall, the manual labeling process made me realize that immigration legislation rarely does exactly one thing. Laws bundle together enforcement provisions and humanitarian provisions all the time, and deciding which direction the law is pulling requires reading it as a complete object with political context, not just parsing its individual sentences.

## Section 3: How Computation Played a Role

The computational part of this project had two steps, and I want to be honest about both of them because I think the details matter for understanding what the model is actually doing. The first step was turning the text of each law into numbers, which is what TF-IDF does. TF-IDF stands for Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency, and the way it works is that it looks at every word across all the law descriptions and asks: how often does this word appear in this specific law, compared to how often it appears across all the other laws? Words that show up in almost every law — like "provides" or "section" — get a very low weight because they do not help distinguish one law from another. Words that appear a lot in one specific law but rarely in others get a high weight because they are doing the real work of describing what that law is about. For immigration laws, that means words like "sanctuary," "ICE," "enforcement," "deportation," and "undocumented" end up carrying a lot of weight, since those are the words that actually tell you what kind of law you are looking at.

The second step was logistic regression, which takes all those TF-IDF scores and learns which combinations of words tend to go with Pro-Immigration laws and which combinations tend to go with Anti-Immigration laws. I trained it on the 30 laws I had labeled myself — 21 Pro-Immigration and 9 Anti-Immigration — and then let it predict labels for the rest. What I found most interesting about the model was the feature weights it learned, because those show you which words it considers the most predictive. The top Pro-Immigration words were things like "school," "victim," "agents," "information," "federal," and phrases like "immigration enforcement" in the context of limiting it — which makes sense because a lot of the pro-immigration laws I labeled were about protecting students, victims of crime, and other vulnerable groups from enforcement. The top Anti-Immigration words included "arizona," "statutes," "data," "training," and phrases like "arizona revised" — which is partly a real pattern (Arizona passed more restrictive laws) and partly a quirk of the training data, since several of my labeled Anti-Immigration laws happened to be Arizona bills and the model picked up on the state name as a signal.

I also want to be clear about what text the model was actually trained on, because this is something I thought about a lot and I want to be transparent about it. The instructor's feedback on my initial dataset was that I should perform sentiment analysis on the law text directly, not on an AI-generated summary, and I took that seriously. But I also did not have full bill text for every single law in the dataset. For 59 laws I had access to the full enrolled bill text retrieved from NCSL and Statenet — the actual legislative language, sometimes twenty pages long. For the remaining Illinois and California laws the model was trained on the LegiScan bill description, which is a one-to-three sentence summary written by the legislative tracking service, not generated by an AI. That is an important distinction. LegiScan descriptions are written by people who work in legislative tracking and they reflect the actual content of the bill, they are just shorter. For the 285 Arizona Legislature rows that came from the azleg.gov API, I only had the bill title — phrases like "illegal aliens; trespassing; enforcement" or "immigration verification; public benefits; asylum" — which is not enough text for TF-IDF to work with meaningfully, and I explain more about that in the limitations section.

To measure how reliable the model was, I used Leave-One-Out cross-validation, which means I trained the model once for each labeled law, each time leaving that one law out and checking whether the model correctly classified it based on the remaining labels. I did it this way rather than the standard train-test split because 30 labeled examples is still a small dataset and I did not want to sacrifice any of them to a held-out test set. The LOO accuracy came out to 80.0 percent, meaning 24 out of 30 training instances were correctly classified when left out of training. That number told me two things: that my labels were internally consistent enough for the model to learn from, and that the model was generalizing from vocabulary patterns rather than just memorizing individual examples. What validated the model most for me was looking at which laws it got wrong. The six misclassified laws — including AZ SR 1036 (a non-binding resolution about ICE policy), CA S 836 (an evidence law about immigration status in court proceedings), AZ S 1350 (a terrorism expansion tied to immigration), AZ H 2162 (the SB 1070 racial profiling amendment), and two 2026 Arizona bills — are exactly the laws I struggled most to label myself. The model struggles precisely where I struggled, which tells me it is picking up on the same vocabulary signals I was responding to when I read the descriptions. The laws that are easy to label from a sentence or two are easy for the model too. The laws that required reading context and political history are the ones both I and the model got wrong.

That does not mean Arizona became pro-immigration — it means the most visible anti-immigration legislation had already passed, and the later sessions were mostly maintaining or modifying what was already on the books. One of my original curiosities was exactly when Arizona became more of an anti-immigration state, and the answer the data gives is: it already was before my dataset starts in 2009, and the 2009–2011 period was when that stance was written most explicitly into law.

## Section 5: What the Data Conceals — Limitations

One of the more important limitations I found during this project was that the TF-IDF method does not work equally well for every source I used. When I built the Arizona State Legislature scraper, I was able to access all 18 legislative sessions from 2009 to 2026 through the Arizona Legislature's own public API, which returned structured data for every bill introduced in each session. The problem was that the API only returns short bill titles — phrases like "immigration; law enforcement; repeal" or "deportation task force; appropriation" — not the full descriptions that my NCSL and LegiScan bills had. TF-IDF works by finding meaningful vocabulary patterns across documents. When a document is four to six words long, there is almost no vocabulary to work with, and the model ends up assigning near-coin-flip probability scores, usually somewhere between 0.50 and 0.54, to essentially every Arizona Legislature bill. That means 89% of those bills came out labeled "Pro-Immigration" not because they are pro-immigration, but because the model had nothing to distinguish them and defaulted to the majority class. A bill like "SB1444 — Deportation task force; appropriation" was scored 0.50, which means the model was genuinely guessing. That is not a finding. That is noise.

I want to be clear that this is not a problem with the Arizona Legislature data itself. The source is public, well-organized, and covers years that other sources like LegiScan and NCSL do not always reach. The problem is that short titles and TF-IDF are a methodological mismatch. The method needs text, and titles are not text in any meaningful sense for this purpose. That is why I kept the Arizona Legislature bills in the dataset for completeness — they represent real legislation that was introduced in real sessions — but I excluded them from the state-level pro-immigration scoring that I use to answer my research question. The state averages I report are based only on the laws that had enough text for the model to actually read, which means the NCSL and LegiScan bills where the descriptions were full sentences. That is a limitation I accept because I think it is more honest than presenting a number I know is wrong.

Also related to this is the broader issue of text quality across the whole dataset, which is something I think is worth being honest about. Across all 589 laws, only 59 had full enrolled bill text — the actual legislative language that a lawyer would read. Those 59 came from NCSL and Statenet, and they are mostly California and Illinois laws where I was able to follow the link to the full document. The rest of the dataset runs on LegiScan descriptions, which are one to three sentences written by a legislative tracking service — not AI-generated, but not the full law either. What I want to be clear about is that even a one-sentence LegiScan description is usually enough for TF-IDF to make a reasonable prediction, because the description almost always names the key policy action — "prohibits law enforcement from cooperating with ICE" or "requires employment eligibility verification for unauthorized aliens" — and those phrases carry strong signal. But it is still a limitation because nuance is lost. A law that does ten things at once gets flattened into one sentence, and the sentence the database writes might emphasize a different aspect than the one that matters most for the pro-versus-anti classification. I found this most often with California bills that mixed housing provisions with immigration status protections, where the description would focus on the housing piece and the immigration piece would be secondary.

## Section 6: Ethical and Privacy Considerations

One thing I want to say first is that the data I collected in this project is all public record. Every law in my dataset was passed by a state legislature, signed or vetoed by a governor, and entered into a publicly maintained database. There are no individual names, no personal addresses, no private documents of any kind. So in the traditional sense of data privacy — protecting people from having their personal information used without their knowledge — this project does not raise those concerns. Still, working with immigration law as data does raise a different kind of ethical question that I think is worth discussing honestly.

The most significant ethical issue in this project is the one built into the labeling itself, which is that I decided what counts as Pro-Immigration and what counts as Anti-Immigration, and that decision reflects a specific point of view. My rubric was based on whether a law expands rights and protections for immigrants or restricts them. That framing assumes that expanding rights is good and that restrictions are bad, which is not a neutral position. There are people who would genuinely argue that enforcement laws serve the public interest, that border security is a legitimate function of government, and that my dataset is simply tracking those policy choices from one side. I do not share that view, but I think it is important to say out loud that my labeling is a perspective, not a fact. Any model trained on my labels inherits that perspective. That is not necessarily wrong — researchers bring values to their data all the time — but it should be stated clearly so that anyone who uses this dataset or this model understands what assumptions are baked into it.

Also, I want to be honest about the potential for the model to be wrong in ways that matter. A logistic regression model trained on 30 examples is not a clinical tool. If someone read my model's predictions and used them to make a claim like "92 percent of Arizona's immigration laws are pro-immigration," that would be a misuse of the results, because those scores include 183 rows where the model had almost no text to work with and was essentially guessing. That is why I was careful throughout this essay to explain which rows the analysis is actually based on and to flag clearly when a number reflects noise rather than signal. The ethical responsibility is not just to collect data cleanly but to report it honestly, including reporting its limits.

One more thing I thought about while building this dataset was who this analysis could affect. Immigration law research that labels states as more or less pro-immigration has a political valence. I am not publishing a policy report, and this is a class project, but I still thought it was worth noting that the way I frame Arizona's anti-immigration period could reinforce existing narratives about that state. I tried to be specific — pointing to 2009 to 2011 as the peak enforcement period rather than characterizing Arizona as monolithically anti-immigration for all time — because specificity is more honest and also more fair to the complexity of what actually happened in that legislature.

## Section 7: How Scale Shaped the Data Over the Semester

The dataset started very small. At the beginning of the semester, I had 67 laws that I built entirely by hand from the NCSL database — going through Illinois, California, and Arizona one session at a time and copying the laws that seemed relevant to immigration enforcement, protections, or immigrant status. That number was enough to understand the shape of the research question, but it was not enough to say anything meaningful statistically. Sixty-seven laws spread across three states and seventeen years meant that some years had only one or two data points, and the model was essentially being asked to make predictions based on patterns it could barely see.

The first scaling attempt was LegiScan, which I registered for because it covers all fifty states going back many years and has a structured API that returns full bill text and metadata. I had a Python script ready to run, and the free tier allows thirty thousand queries per month, which would have been more than enough. The problem was that the API key did not arrive in my email right away. That waiting period cost me several days of data collection time. When the key did arrive, I ran bulk fetches across two keyword sets — one broad set covering general immigration terms, and one narrow set focused on the three states — and ended up with 337 rows that had full bill descriptions from LegiScan. Those 337 rows were the core of the scaling push, because LegiScan descriptions are full sentences rather than titles, which means the TF-IDF model had vocabulary to actually work with.

The second scaling push was for California specifically. The California legislative portal at leginfo.legislature.ca.gov goes back to 1999, which is earlier than LegiScan covered for our needs, and I wanted to extend the California timeline further back. The site uses a JavaServer Faces form technology that does not work with a standard Python HTTP library — the browser JavaScript secretly changes the form's POST target before submitting, and replicating that in Python turned out to be much harder than expected. After several debugging attempts that returned zero results because of session cookie conflicts, I switched to using curl subprocesses instead of Python requests, and that finally worked. The scraper pulled 895 California bills across six sessions from 1999 to 2010 using eight immigration-related keywords. Not all 895 were genuinely immigration-related — some matched on "e-verify" for unrelated reasons like farm license verification — but the core immigration bills were there, and they extended the California coverage back another decade.

For Arizona, the scaling story is different and honestly more limited. The Arizona Legislature has a public JSON API at apps.azleg.gov that returns bill data by bill number and session year, and I was able to use it to retrieve the bill title and NOW title — which is the amended title that shows what the bill actually became — for all 183 Arizona rows that linked to the azleg.gov portal. What I could not get was a full multi-sentence description for any of those bills. The API only returns titles, not summaries. That is a limitation I accepted because the alternative was leaving those 183 rows with no text at all, and having the title is better than having nothing for the TF-IDF model even if the vocabulary is minimal.

So by the end, the dataset had grown from 67 to 589 rows, but the scaling was not uniform. The Illinois and California rows that came from LegiScan have full paragraph descriptions. The Arizona azleg rows have three-to-ten word titles. That gap in description quality meant that when I scored all 589 rows, the model was effectively working with two different kinds of text — rich descriptions for one subset and sparse titles for another — and the scores for the sparse-title Arizona rows ended up clustering near 0.50, which is the model saying it genuinely cannot tell. That is why the state-level averages in my final analysis are based on the rows with enough text for the model to actually read, rather than on the full 589 rows. The scaling increased the volume of the dataset, but it did not solve the underlying data quality problem for the Arizona legislative portal rows.

## Section 8: Lessons Learned

The most important thing I learned from this project is that data quality matters more than data volume. I started the semester thinking that getting to a larger number of rows was the primary goal — that if I could move from 67 laws to several hundred, the analysis would automatically become more reliable and more interesting. And the dataset did grow, from 67 to 589 rows, which I am proud of because that scaling required real technical work. But when I ran the classifier on all 589 rows, I found that the 183 Arizona laws with only three-to-six word titles produced near-coin-flip scores that told me nothing. So I ended up doing the meaningful analysis on a subset anyway — the rows where I had enough text for the model to actually read. That is a lesson I will carry into future data work: ask first whether the data you have is the right kind, before asking whether you have enough of it.

I also learned a lot about the gap between a plan and its execution. Before I started building the scraper for the California leginfo portal, I thought the hard part would be finding the right URL and figuring out which fields to extract. The actual hard part was that the site uses JavaServer Faces technology, which means the browser secretly changes the form's POST target before submitting, and a standard Python HTTP request could not replicate that. I spent more time debugging the California scraper than I spent on any other single piece of the project, and the fix — switching from Python requests to curl subprocesses — was not obvious until after several failed attempts. That experience taught me that web scraping is fundamentally a process of understanding how a specific site works, not just a matter of applying a general pattern. Every site is different, and the differences matter.

Also, I found that the human labeling process was more intellectually demanding than I expected. I thought I would be able to get through the 30 labeled laws quickly since I was reading one or two sentences per law. But the borderline cases — the laws that bundle enforcement and protection together, the laws where the title sounds neutral but the political context makes the intent clear — those required me to do real research. For AZ H 2162, the SB 1070 amendment, I read about the legal history of SB 1070, looked at the timeline of lawsuits against it, and tried to understand what "clarifying reasonable suspicion requirements" was actually doing in practice before I felt confident labeling it. That is the kind of close reading that computational methods cannot replace, and I think the essay is better for having done it seriously rather than making quick calls on every law.

One thing that worked very well was the Leave-One-Out cross-validation approach. Since I only had 30 labeled examples, I did not want to sacrifice any of them to a held-out test set. LOO CV let me get a reliable estimate of model accuracy — 80.0 percent — without losing any training data, and the pattern of which laws it got wrong gave me confidence that the model was learning something real. The six misclassified laws were the same ones I found hardest to label myself, which means the model is picking up on genuine vocabulary ambiguity, not random noise. For small datasets, LOO CV is genuinely the right tool, and I am glad I knew about it from the course material rather than defaulting to a train-test split that would have given me less reliable results with fewer examples.

The last lesson is about the relationship between computation and judgment. I went into this project a little worried that using a machine learning model would undermine the integrity of the labeling — that I was outsourcing a decision that should be mine. But what I found in practice is that the model and the human labeling are doing two completely different things. My labels are the ground truth that the model learns from. The model's predictions are a scaling tool that extends those labels to rows I could not manually read. They work together, and neither one alone would give me the result I have. What the model is really doing is asking: given the vocabulary patterns in the laws Daria labeled, does this new law look more like the ones she called Pro or the ones she called Anti? That framing made me less worried about the model doing something I could not see and more confident about what it was actually doing.

## Section 9: Situating the Work in Scholarship

One of the central ideas in this course was that culture can be treated as data — that the things people make and do and legislate leave traces that can be read computationally as well as humanly. The concept that I kept coming back to while building this project is what Franco Moretti called "distant reading," which is the idea that you can learn things about a body of texts by analyzing them computationally at scale that you simply cannot see when you read each one closely. Moretti applied this to literary history, arguing that you cannot understand the novel as a cultural form by reading a hundred canonical novels — you have to look at the thousands of novels that were written and forgotten, because the patterns are in the mass, not just in the canonized examples. I was not working with novels, but I was working with the same basic problem: there are hundreds of immigration laws spread across three states and seventeen years, and no single human reader can hold all of them in mind at once and say where the patterns are. The TF-IDF model is my distant reading tool. It does not read any law the way I read a law, but it does find vocabulary patterns across all 589 rows that I could not find by going through them one at a time.

At the same time, this project also confirmed for me why distant reading has to be paired with close reading. When I was doing the labeling, I found that the laws that were hardest to classify were exactly the ones where the surface vocabulary does not tell you anything useful. The AZ H 2162 amendment to SB 1070 does not use words like "deportation" or "enforcement" prominently — it talks about "reasonable suspicion" and "prohibition on racial profiling," which sound protective. Without reading the political and legal context, a computational model would likely score that law as Pro-Immigration, because the language sounds like a rights protection. And in fact that is exactly how I labeled it in the end — Pro-Immigration, because the racial profiling prohibition does create a real legal constraint on officers, even if the political motivation for writing it was to protect SB 1070. The model predicted Anti-Immigration for that one in LOO validation, which makes it one of the six misclassifications. That disagreement between me and the model is itself meaningful — it shows where the vocabulary stops being sufficient and judgment has to take over. That is the kind of distinction that only comes from close reading, and it is also why my 30 manually labeled laws had to carry real intellectual work, not just quick categorizations.

The use of logistic regression for classifying political texts has a long history in political science and computational linguistics. Researchers have used bag-of-words methods and TF-IDF combined with logistic regression to classify legislative documents, party manifestos, and judicial opinions going back to the 1990s. What I was doing in this project is a small-scale version of that tradition — using vocabulary frequency patterns to place political texts along an ideological dimension. What is different about my project is that the training labels are mine, not a pre-existing partisan coding scheme, and the dimension I am classifying along is specific to immigration policy rather than left-right ideology in general. That specificity is both a strength and a weakness: a strength because it lets me capture distinctions that a generic liberal-conservative classifier would miss, and a weakness because my labels are idiosyncratic and a different researcher applying the same rubric might draw some of the borderline cases differently.

I also want to situate this work in relation to the data ethics frameworks we read in this course, particularly around the question of whose perspective gets encoded in a dataset. My dataset encodes my perspective — a perspective that treats expanding immigrant rights as the normative good and enforcement expansion as the normative bad. That is not the only defensible perspective on immigration policy, and I am aware of it. What I tried to do throughout this project was be explicit about that framing rather than hiding it behind the apparent objectivity of numbers. The model's scores look like objective measurements — 0.527 for California, 0.535 for Illinois — but they are only meaningful relative to the labels I assigned, which are themselves value-laden decisions. That is true of most machine learning applications, and one of the things this course helped me understand is that calling something a "data-driven" result does not make it neutral. It just means the value choices were made earlier in the process and are harder to see.
