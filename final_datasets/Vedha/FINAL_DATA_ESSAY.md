**Final Data Essay: Culture as Dataset, Culture as Documentation**

This project began as an attempt to understand American political history through visual culture. My original idea was to build a dataset of about 75 works of art from 1750 to 2020 that shaped or reflected political moments in the United States. I treated paintings, photographs, posters, murals, and memorial art as political artifacts rather than aesthetic objects. The goal was to examine how visual culture participates in political life and becomes part of public memory.

At the beginning, I designed a detailed schema including title, artist, year, medium, political context, and a political impact rating from one to five. I also included race and gender of the artist and a significance field where I wrote interpretive notes about each work. This early structure was influenced by the Responsible Datasets in Context idea that “data cannot be analyzed responsibly without deep knowledge of its social and historical context, provenance, and limitations.” I initially aimed to encode as much context as possible into structured form, but this also made clear how quickly interpretation becomes embedded in data design.

To build the dataset, I combined manual research with computational tools. I wrote a Python scraper to collect metadata from museum APIs, including the Art Institute of Chicago, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Cleveland Museum of Art, and the Harvard Art Museums. The scraper gathered fields such as title, date, medium, and object links into a CSV file. I then manually cleaned and expanded entries using museum descriptions and historical sources. Even at this stage, it was clear that structured cultural data is not neutral. Every field required interpretive decisions, especially when defining what counted as political relevance.

Instructor feedback significantly reshaped the project. The historical scope was too broad, spanning over two centuries, which made consistent interpretation difficult. The political impact rating also conflated circulation with political effect, and the race and ethnicity field risked imposing unstable categories across historical periods. I was also encouraged to include direct links to artworks for transparency and verification.

In response, I narrowed the dataset mainly to the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, with limited eighteenth century material. This created a more coherent framework for comparing movements such as abolitionism, industrialization, labor activism, civil rights, and early political propaganda. I removed the race and ethnicity column and the impact rating, shifting instead toward descriptive historical context. I also added direct links to museum pages, improving traceability and allowing readers to engage directly with source material. This aligned with course ideas about provenance, where datasets should make visible how they are constructed and accessed.

The computational workflow also evolved. While I initially experimented with AI tools for annotation, I ultimately restricted computation to retrieval and cleaning. The scraper handled pagination, normalized fields across different museum schemas, and merged outputs into a unified CSV format. Metadata logs preserved provenance and reproducibility. This separation clarified an important distinction: computation supported scale, but interpretation remained a human responsibility, especially for culturally sensitive material.

Computation played a central role in shaping both scale and interpretation. The scraper acted as a translation layer between institutional APIs and a unified dataset structure. It determined what could be included based on what was searchable, accessible, and returned by museum systems. Early interpretation lived in the significance field, but later it shifted upstream into query design, API selection, and keyword framing. Even choosing search terms such as “civil war,” “revolution,” or “labor” shaped which objects entered the dataset.

Scale changed the structure of knowledge produced by the dataset. The initial 75 entry version allowed for detailed interpretive writing for each object. Expanding to 664 records required standardization across fields such as artist_display, medium, and department, which reduced space for narrative depth. The dataset shifted from close reading toward comparative structure, reflecting a central tension in IS 310 between interpretive detail and large scale analysis. Raw_source fields were preserved to retain depth, but analytical emphasis moved toward patterns across institutions.

The final dataset has important limitations. It depends on four museum APIs and therefore reflects institutional acquisition histories rather than the full spectrum of cultural production. Protest art, street murals, and community produced visual culture are significantly underrepresented. It also inherits biases from museum metadata, including uneven cataloging practices and missing or inconsistent records. Because it is search driven, results are shaped by query language before data collection even begins.

Ethical questions also shaped the project. Removing race and ethnicity fields was intended to avoid anachronistic classification across historical periods, though it reduced the ability to analyze structural inequality directly. Linking to museum pages rather than rehosting images respected licensing constraints and maintained transparency of provenance. These decisions reflect tradeoffs between analytical depth, ethical caution, and institutional constraints.

Across the project, key lessons from IS 310 became concrete. Data is not discovered but constructed through decisions about inclusion, exclusion, and categorization. As Catherine D’Ignazio and Lauren Klein argue in *Data Feminism*, “numbers are never neutral,” and this is visible in how museum systems determine what becomes searchable. Dorothy Berry’s work on archival infrastructure further explains how institutional cataloging shapes what cultural materials are preserved and made visible. The Responsible Datasets in Context framework reinforced that documentation is part of the dataset itself, not an external layer added after the fact.

Ultimately, this project shows that cultural datasets are shaped as much by computation and infrastructure as by historical content. The scraper, API systems, and cleaning pipeline did not simply scale the dataset but reshaped what could be seen in the first place. What began as a close interpretive archive of politically significant artworks became a structured view of what major museums make digitally accessible. The final dataset reflects both cultural history and the limits of its representation, showing how data is produced through layered interpretive, technical, and institutional decisions.
