Final Data Essay: Culture as Dataset, Culture as Documentation

This project began as an attempt to understand American political history through visual culture. My original idea was to build a dataset of about 75 works of art from 1750 to 2020 that shaped or reflected political moments in the United States. I treated paintings, photographs, posters, murals, and memorial art as political artifacts rather than just aesthetic objects. The goal was to ask how visual culture participates in political life and how it becomes part of public memory.

At the beginning, I designed a very detailed dataset structure. Each entry included fields such as title, artist, year, medium, political context, and a political impact rating from one to five. I also included categories like race and gender of the artist, and a significance field where I wrote interpretive notes about why each piece mattered. This early version was very influenced by the idea in the Responsible Datasets in Context project that “data cannot be analyzed responsibly without deep knowledge of its social and historical context, provenance, and limitations.” I tried to capture as much context as possible in structured form.

As I started building the dataset, I used a combination of manual research and computational tools. I wrote a simple scraper to pull metadata from online museum catalogs and public archives such as the Smithsonian and Library of Congress. The scraper helped me collect basic information like title, date, and object links into a csv file. I then manually cleaned and expanded the entries by checking each item and adding context from museum descriptions and historical sources. This process showed me early that even structured cultural data is not neutral. Every field required interpretation, especially when deciding what counted as political relevance.

During the semester, feedback from my instructor changed the direction of the project in important ways. One major suggestion was that my timeline was too broad. Covering more than two hundred years of American history made it difficult to maintain depth and consistency. Another suggestion was that my political impact rating was unclear because it mixed together influence, circulation, and interpretation. I also received feedback that my race and ethnicity field could be anachronistic and misleading when applied across historical periods. Finally, I was encouraged to include links to the original artworks so that readers could verify and visually engage with the data.

Based on this feedback, I revised the dataset significantly. I narrowed the historical focus mainly to the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, with only a few entries from the eighteenth century. This made the dataset more coherent and easier to compare across movements like abolitionism, industrialization, labor activism, civil rights, and early modern political propaganda. I also removed the race and ethnicity column and the political impact rating. Instead of trying to quantify impact, I shifted toward describing historical context and circulation patterns in text form when relevant. This change helped reduce over interpretation and made the dataset more transparent.

Another major change was the inclusion of direct links to museum pages or archival sources for each artwork. This was important because it grounded each dataset entry in an external reference point. It also reduced the burden on the reader, who no longer needs to search for each artwork separately. This change also aligned with one of the core ideas from the course readings that datasets should make their provenance visible. In the Responsible Datasets in Context framework, provenance is not just about where data comes from but also about how it is transformed through each step of collection and structuring.

The computational part of the project also evolved. Initially, I experimented with using AI tools to help generate descriptions and fill in missing metadata. However, I decided not to use automated annotation for interpretive fields after reflecting on instructor feedback and the limitations of model generated interpretation. Instead, computation was used mainly for data collection and organization. The scraper helped gather structured metadata, and Python scripts helped clean inconsistent formats and merge entries into a single csv dataset. This separation between collection and interpretation became important. It showed me that automation can support scale but should not replace interpretive judgment in culturally sensitive fields.

One of the key lessons from the course is that data is always constructed rather than found. In Critical Computing and Cultural Data, we discussed how datasets are shaped by decisions about inclusion, exclusion, and categorization. This project made that idea very concrete. For example, deciding what counts as political art required constant judgment. Some works clearly relate to events like protests or wars, while others gain political meaning only through later interpretation. I had to decide whether to include only works with direct political intent or also works that became political over time.

Another important concept from the course is that scale changes meaning. When I first imagined the dataset, I thought scaling up would simply make it more complete. But working with data showed me that scale also changes what can be seen. A small dataset allows for detailed interpretive writing for each entry. A larger dataset requires simplification and standardization, which can remove nuance. This tension appeared clearly when I moved from manual entry toward scraper assisted collection. The more structured the dataset became, the more I had to reduce complexity in individual entries.

There are also limitations in this dataset that reflect broader issues in digital cultural archives. Most of the data comes from institutional sources such as museums and national archives. This means that well documented and preserved works are overrepresented, while community based or ephemeral political art is underrepresented. Even with scraping, the dataset still reflects what has been digitized and categorized by institutions rather than what existed historically in everyday spaces like streets or protest sites.

Ethically, this project raised questions about representation and interpretation. Removing the race and ethnicity column was one response to the risk of imposing modern categories on historical figures. At the same time, it reduced the ability to analyze patterns of exclusion in art history. This tradeoff reflects a broader challenge in cultural data work, where adding structure can both reveal and obscure meaning.

Overall, this project showed me how cultural data is made through a combination of technical tools and interpretive decisions. The scraper, csv structure, and cleaning scripts were important for managing scale, but the most important work remained in deciding what each entry meant and why it mattered. The final dataset is not a complete record of American political art, but a structured interpretation of selected works across two centuries. It reflects both what is present in institutional archives and what is missing from them, and it demonstrates how data is always shaped by the choices of the person building it.
