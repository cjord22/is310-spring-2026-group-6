Final Data Essay: Culture as Dataset, Culture as Documentation


My semester-long project began with an uncomfortably expansive question carried by deceptively simple objects: how visual culture becomes politically consequential. For the initial bespoke dataset of approximately 75 entries, I treated each row as something closer to intellectual history rather than décor. I chased works—prints, cartoons, posters, murals, and memorials—that had entered public argument, censorship fights, campaigning, mass reproduction, court-adjacent visual memory, or the texture of grassroots movements.That first phase disciplined me into the mundane truth repeated across IS 310: datasets are authored, not harvested from nature. This realization directly reflects the course description's emphasis on culture as interpretation and the presence of infrastructural bias. My spreadsheet made visible what “political relevance” threatened to mean whenever I keyed it into categorical fields.Yet that exact richness could not ethically or practically scale the way a naive imagination suggests. Instructor feedback persuaded me not to overstretch geography and chronology simultaneously, not to stabilize race in a brittle column sensitive to temporal slippage, and not to disguise circulation as measurable political impact. I narrowed the historical frame—emphasizing the long nineteenth and early twentieth centuries with a restrained number of Revolutionary-era anchors—and redesigned the augmentation stage around museum open-access APIs.The final dataset contains 664 records across four major sources: the Art Institute of Chicago, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Cleveland Museum of Art, and the Harvard Art Museums. The final artifact is therefore deliberately split in spirit: legacy interpretive instincts from the bespoke pass now meet a computational ingestion layer that treats major museums as partial compilers rather than impartial mirrors. What follows explains how computation entered the lifecycle, why scale rewrote affordances I had prized in prose fields, where responsibility still sits with the curator-researcher, and how IS 310 readings on archives, enumeration, scraping, APIs, cleaning, and the Responsible Datasets in Context (RDC) project frame how I narrate concealment—not as embarrassment, but as epistemic hygiene.2. Bespoke Beginnings: Interpreting Without Pretending to Quantify PoliticsEarly on, I organized fields like historical_event, themes, and a long prose significance column keyed to causal stories and controversies. These were not only "facts," but ethically thick footnotes folded into CSV cells: examples included Paul Revere plagiarizing Henry Pelham's representation of the Boston Massacre, the complex visual afterlives of Dorothea Lange’s Florence Owens Thompson photograph, and the revelation that Ernest Withers doubled as an activist photographer and an FBI asset. Computation still mattered immediately: Claude became a glorified bibliography engine—fast at checking dates, correcting obvious slip-ups where museum catalog copy was searchable, and widening context when I anchored it with primary sources afterward.That computational assistance amplified a familiar danger from discussions of augmentation and AI: models reward well-documented canons disproportionately. The more digitized institutional memory that exists for a subject, the more confident the completions become. Entries tied to marginalized traditions required slower human triangulation precisely because catalogs and secondary digitization pipelines themselves skew.This stage taught me, in the spirit of Catherine D’Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein’s injunction that numbers never speak alone, that subjective ratings need explicit operationalizations or they mislead future readers pretending comparability. Instructor feedback clarified that my numeric "impact" variable blurred reach with effects. Consequently, I phased that variable out alongside an artist race/ethnicity field when I reorganized downstream files, aligning with critiques against stabilizing unstable identity categories retrospectively.3. Instructor Feedback: Pivoting the QuestionRather than brute-forcing thematic closure over 250 years—a scope that inevitably amplified sampling bias—the refined question became: What does searchable museum metadata make legible, especially when we purposely aim queries at loosely "American-history" linguistic hooks?This is no retreat from politics; it is a methodological relocation. Institutional APIs already embed curatorial genealogies ("who decided what belonged and how descriptions read"), tying directly to Dorothy Berry’s argument that archival architectures channel whose culture gets durable metadata bandwidth. My political reading must now grapple with infrastructural layering: first my interpretive scaffolding, and later each museum’s ingestion pipeline.Critically, instructor guidance to include authoritative links and references surfaced a gap that API aggregation still struggles to unify: heterogeneous image rights and IIIF quirks. For instance, certain rows returned empty thumbnails despite supposedly image-positive searches—a reminder that openness is granular, not categorical.4. Computation at Scale: The Scraper as a Transparent Translation LayerTo address the revised research question, I constructed a Python script to aggregate data from four primary targets: the Art Institute of Chicago (where a public domain filter is available), the Metropolitan Museum of Art (requiring search queries followed by iterative object requests), the Cleveland Museum of Art (utilizing their CC0 Open Access channel), and the Harvard Art Museums (using a keyed institutional search endpoint).The scraper normalizes these records into 16 unified columns: uid, source, source_object_id, title, artist_display, date_display, medium, culture, department, description, image_url, iiif_image_id, license_or_rights, object_url, search_queries, and raw_source. These columns enforce multi-repository comparability, but they also flatly homogenize separate semantic worlds. I logged the metadata for each execution in final_american_history_meta.json so that provenance survives. Temporary errors, such as brief HTTP 403 request bursts from the Met API, annotate the operational limits of treating interfaces as hospitable commons.Weekly trajectories—moving from scraping ecology drills to structured HTTP GET and API exercises—taught me that scale is never merely a matter of clicking "run." It demands pacing, exponential backoff, reproducible notebooks, and disciplined documentation. This technical rigor mirrors the course’s culminating push toward reproducible, Responsible-Dataset-style narration.Furthermore, cleaning decisions became an explicit confession. Dropping fields like description and culture from the active spreadsheet view after normalization maintained tabular legibility, while preserving the full payloads within the raw_source column for auditing. This directly echoes our "cleaning and curating" module: cleaning is never innocent erasure; it is selective memorialization.5. What the Dataset Now Represents (And Why That Claim Is Narrow)The completed table of 664 rows is not a roster of the most politically consequential American images of the long nineteenth century. It is better glossed as a cross-section of objects surfacing when multiple major Anglophone museum discovery layers answer repeated keyword probes oriented to “American history.”This systemic boundary means an over-representation of institutional acquisition paths, English metadata, objects whose photography already exists, and anything that internal search engines rank highly for those specific lexical phrases. The collection includes notable frequencies of major figures, such as 15 works by Winslow Homer and 14 by George Inness, alongside 62 pieces from unidentified creators. The text search parameters generated structural false positives (such as name collisions in artist metadata) and false negatives (such as street ephemera, censored community artifacts, or anything never formally accessioned by these institutions). This epistemic modesty mirrors the RDC insistence that responsible analysis demands a clear understanding of the historical and social constraints of production, which I have front-loaded into the project repository README.6. Ethical ContoursThe ethical execution of this scale expansion rests on four key coordinates:Rights and Reproducibility: Image URLs lack legal clearance for downstream republication unless the target object page explicitly confirms public domain or Creative Commons allowances.Privacy: Named individuals in captions sit in openly published catalogs; humane reuse avoids speculative biographical extrapolation unsupported by primary sources.The Political Neutrality Myth: Selecting initial queries ("Revolution", "Lincoln", "Civil War") pre-figures the narrative possibility spaces. Queries are tacit hypotheses.Technical Courtesy: Implementing controlled API pacing—rather than executing burst requests—is more than politeness; it prevents degrading shared network access for peers.8. Scholarly AnchorsThis project moves beyond anecdotal observation by anchoring its arguments in core critical texts. Catherine D’Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein’s Data Feminism provides the framework for examining power, counting, and interpretation, particularly through their analysis of what gets counted. The Responsible Datasets in Context exemplar by Sylvia Fernández Trejo and Paulina Hernandez, “Gender Violence at the Border (1993–1998),” models how to layer documentation to reveal institutional gaps. Finally, Dorothy Berry’s “The House Archives Built” frames the structural biases inherent in legacy archival architectures. Together, these texts support my insistence that the dataset's README and metadata manifest collectively perform an argument rather than serving as accessory decorations.9. What Remains ConcealedSignificant structural absences linger by design and infrastructure. Murals and protest graphics remain heavily under-documented compared to traditional canvas paintings. Nineteenth-century marginalized makers are frequently under-described linguistically inside legacy authority files, and multilingual political visuality is largely filtered out unless anglicized terminology triggers a keyword hit. Dropping highly variable columns from the immediate spreadsheet view improved analytical usability, but it outsourced nuance retrieval to investigative readers willing to decompress the raw JSON payloads.10. Growth Narrative and ConclusionMy understanding of computation evolved from viewing it as a supplementary fact-checking assistant to recognizing it as an orchestrated, reproducible engine for ingestion and normalization. Intellectual growth fused affective skepticism (refusing to implicitly trust charismatic rating integers) with infrastructural skepticism (interrogating retrieval interfaces as cultural gatekeepers fashioned through acquisition budgets and colonial legacies).The hardest lesson learned is that augmenting a project ethically is less about accumulating massive row counts than broadcasting explicit uncertainty bookkeeping tied to reproducible tooling. A hybrid analytical road remains open: future work can select high-stakes strata for renewed manual interpretive essay fields while reserving API passes for exploratory macro-patterns. This division of labor honors the caution that quantification complements rather than substitutes for historical reasoning.
